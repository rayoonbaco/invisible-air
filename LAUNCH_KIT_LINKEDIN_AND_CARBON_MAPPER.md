# Invisible Air — Focused Launch Kit

## LinkedIn launch post

I spent the last eight months learning how far one person can push AI-assisted product development when the goal is not another chatbot, but a real decision instrument.

Today I am sharing **Invisible Air: Atmospheric Observatory**.

It is an interactive, material-aware atmospheric reasoning prototype. A visitor can place or move multiple sources, retrieve location-specific atmospheric inputs, see independently modeled influence fields overlap or separate, and receive a deterministic explanation of both **what changed** and **why the model changed**. Each experiment can now be preserved and reopened in an Experiment Journal.

The project is intentionally careful about its limits. It does not claim measured concentration, verified emissions, AQI, exposure, or a wildfire forecast. Visitor-added sources use explicit normalized release assumptions.

What I wanted to prove was broader: AI can help a nontraditional builder design a technically disciplined, human-in-the-loop instrument that makes evidence, assumptions, uncertainty, and model behavior visible.

I am looking for work with teams building applied AI, decision-support products, geospatial intelligence, environmental data tools, agent workflows, or human-centered scientific software.

## Carbon Mapper outreach email

Subject: Independent prototype exploring a human-review layer for atmospheric observations

Hello Carbon Mapper team,

My name is Ray Gomez. I built an independent proof-of-work project inspired by the challenge Carbon Mapper addresses: making complex atmospheric observations accessible and actionable without hiding uncertainty or overstating what the evidence supports.

The prototype is called **Invisible Air: Atmospheric Observatory**. It does not reproduce Carbon Mapper products, claim affiliation, or treat modeled influence as observed methane. Instead, it explores a complementary human-review interaction layer: multiple movable sources, location-specific atmospheric inputs, explicit release assumptions, terrain-aware relative influence fields, deterministic explanations of what changed and why, and an experiment journal that preserves the reasoning path.

I built it to demonstrate how I approach AI-assisted product design, evidence boundaries, human-in-the-loop workflows, and explanatory scientific interfaces. Carbon Mapper’s mission of making methane and CO2 data accessible and actionable strongly aligns with the problem I was trying to understand.

I would be grateful for a brief look and candid feedback—especially on whether this kind of explanatory review surface could complement real observation products or educational workflows. I am also actively looking for opportunities where this combination of product thinking, AI orchestration, and decision-support design could be useful.
